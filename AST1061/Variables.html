INSTRUCTIONS:

Your approach will be to find your variables in the sky with a sky map (like the ones handed out in class) and a finder chart (customized for each variable).  It takes about 15 minutes in the dark to get your eyes dark adapted.  Use a dim flashlight (a red filter helps), otherwise you kill your dark adaption.  Record the information in your observing log (below).  For the altitude and azimuth, try using your fist at arm's length.  You then compare the brightness of your variable star to the stars around it which we call ... comparison stars.  If you can find one star slightly brighter than your variable and one a little fainter, then you will record a magnitude part-way between those two comparison stars.  This is the essense of relative visual photometry.  It is difficult for the eye to perceive differences in brightness between two stars greater than about 0.1 magnitude (you should test this out for your own eyes).  So that will probably be your minimum uncertainty.  However, if you can bracket the variable between two stars that are only 0.2 mag different then you will be assured of an uncertainty smaller than or around 0.1 magnitude.  Be careful choosing your comparison stars;  you don't want to choose a star that is too far away (>20 degrees) from the variable because it may have a different amount of dimming (extinction) by the atmosphere and clouds.  Also, comparing stars that have very different color, or are variables themselves, may give skewed results. 
    Try to make or find finder charts that have your variable in the center, and are approximately 30 degrees across.  Label as many comparison stars as you can with their magnitude.  You can find the magnitudes of stars by clicking on them in Stellarium.  However, Stellarium will not tell you that a star is variable, so you should confirm with another atlas (e.g., Peterson's Field Guide to the Stars and Planets) whether the stars are variable. 
    You should start with 3 variables to observe.  You can find online schedules showing when the minima occur.  An example is: http://www.aavso.org/eclipsing-binary-ephemerides . (Check out the AAVSO website for lots of other info on observing variables).  You want to make some observations during maximum as well as around minimum.  After attempting to measure them for a few weeks, you may find that one is much more difficult to observe than the others.  You can then drop that star and focus on the others.  If the remaining two still seem difficult, you might exchange one with a new star from the table below.
    Once you do detect an eclipse, you should try to make several observations about 30-60 minutes apart.  In particular, if you get a reading both before and after minimum, it is easier to estimate when the minimum occurred.  Note that both beta Lyrae and Delta Cephei are continuously changing, while Algol has long periods of constancy.
    Using binoculars can really enhance this project.   They allow you to see several magnitudes fainter so that you have many more variable stars to choose from. They also make it easier to see the brighter variables when they are near their minima.
