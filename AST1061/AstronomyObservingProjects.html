1. The orbital periods of the Moon.
Description:  You will observe the Moon's phase and position (relative to the stars) around the 1st quarter phase of the Moon for at least two months in a row.  You can then measure for yourself the length of the synodic and sidereal months.  The synodic month is the time between two like phases.  The sidereal month is the time between two passages of the Moon past a given position on the sky (use right ascension because the Moon will not follow the exact same path on consecutive months).  Therefore, you must note the precise phase (% illumination) of the Moon and its position in the sky (plot it on a star map) on the 1st quarter and the 4 days before and after 1st quarter.

2. Variable stars.
Description:  You will monitor 2-3 variable stars in the sky and try to catch them near minima.  The best stars are Beta Lyrae, Algol, and Delta Cephei in the constellations Lyra, Perseus, and Cepheus, respectively.  The first two of these are eclipsing binary star systems, while Delta Cephei is a pulsating star.  Other variables can be found through research, but you should check with your instructor as to whether they will be "doable".   You will need to find published predictions of minima and maxima in order to plan your observations.  You will also need to learn about doing photometry by eye using finder charts and several comparison stars for each variable star.

3. Tracing the Sun's analemma.
Description:  You will record the altitude and azimuth of the Sun at exactly the same time of day for as many days as possible.  Choose a time to record that is within 2 hours of noon. Once a week will probably be a sufficient frequency, but observations should be sustained over about 3 months.  If you have a good method for measuring the Sun's position (a makeshift gnomon, or sundial) you will easily notice the change in altitude of the Sun as Winter progresses into Spring.  But you will also see a change in azimuth.  You will plot your positions on a graph of altitude and azimuth.  (If ambitious, you can try to combine photos into a composite or make an animation.) Try to explain the way the Sun's position changes in terms of the Earth's orbital motion and tilt.

4. The motions of planets and comets
Description:  You will see for yourself why planets were called "wandering stars" - they are bright dots that move relative to the constellations.  You will record the positions of two solar system bodies and try to make sense of their motion over a 2-3 month period.  For spring 2013, Jupiter is the obvious choice, although Saturn will be visible before midnight by mid-February and Mars and Venus are early evening or morning objects.  Also, Comet PANSTARRS (C2011 L4) should reach naked-eye brightness around March/April.  You should begin by observing the two planets with the best visibility (bright and above the horizon at a convenient time).  If a comet becomes naked-eye visible, you can drop the planet which is least visible.  Binoculars (stored at the observatory) can greatly improve the visibility of comets, allowing you to record them even as they have faded from naked-eye view.

5. Weather and the twinkling of stars.
Description:  When stars twinkle, it is not something happening physically to the star, but instead it is related to the inhomogeneities of the Earth's atmosphere.  You will record the weather conditions for every night (don't use forecasts, only current conditions) and observe twinkling at least 2 times per week.   The times should not be closer together than about 1 day.   Then you will look for correlations between the amount of twinkling and the weather.  Is twinkling more extreme when the sky is completely clear or when it is hazy?  Does it occur more when temperatures are high, or low, or when temperatures are climbing or falling?  When is twinkling the least noticeable?
