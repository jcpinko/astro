Project #5:  The Weather and Twinkling
DESCRIPTION:   When stars twinkle, it is not something happening physically to the star, but instead it is related to the inhomogeneities of the Earth's atmosphere.  You will record the weather conditions for every night (use current conditions or forecasts less than 1 day in advance) and observe twinkling at least 2 times per week.   These times should not be closer together than a day unless the weather changes drastically between observations.   Then you will look for correlations between the amount of twinkling and the weather.  Is twinkling more extreme when the sky is completely clear or when it is hazy?  Does it occur more when temperatures are high, or low, or when temperatures are climbing or falling?  When is twinkling the least noticeable?  Since twinkling is hard to quantify, you should also record the seeing forecasts given on this web page:  http://cleardarksky.com/c/ONUObOHkey.html?1 for the nights that you can observe.  Can you see a correlation between seeing and twinkling?

INSTRUCTIONS:

Record the weather for every evening despite whether you go outside on that evening or not.  You want the weather during the hour you plan to make observations.  This should include temperature, temperature rise or fall since previous night, wind, pressure, and change in pressure since previous night. In addition, record the seeing on a scale of 1 to 5, and the cloud cover (in % coverage) using the Clear Sky Chart for ONU (http://cleardarksky.com/c/ONUObOHkey.html?1 ). You should be able to get by without records for every night, but try to get as complete records as possible in order to notice correlations.

Go outside to observe twinkling about 2 times per week.  You should probably plan to check the sky 4-5 nights a week, because about one-half of those nights will be too cloudy.  If it is partly cloudy (<50%), wait for the clouds to pass over your star(s) of interest.  Twinkling is difficult to quantify without instruments.  With a photometer, one could quantify the amplitude of the fluctuations (which could be measured in magnitudes) and also the time between large fluctuations.  You don't have such instruments, but you may still be able to develop a way to characterize the twinkling by eye during the first week or two.  The smallest perceptible brightness difference for the eye is about 0.1 magnitudes. Check out the twinkling setting for the program Stellarium (it goes from 0 to 1.5 and seems to be measured in magnitudes). 

Compare stars at different altitudes above the horizon.  You'll see that twinkling is most noticeable with bright stars at low altitudes, so you must record an altitude estimate for any star that you use.  Planets usually do not twinkle, but they can under extreme conditions, so you could check the planets for twinkling as they can indicate the strongest twinkling conditions.  If the "twinkles" seems to happen just once in awhile, try measuring the time between strong twinkles.  The average time between strong scintillations is one way to characterize them.   Alternatively, you can estimate a frequency of twinkling (twinkles per minute or per second).  Another thing that may differ from one night to the next is the appearance of sporadic colors - a blue star may flash red occasionally.

It is up to you exactly how you do your measurements, but a suggested approach follows.  Use Stellarium to find what time Sirius will be around 15 degrees in altitude.  Find other bright stars which will have different altitudes at that time (e.g., Rigel and Procyon) and note their altitudes.  Go out to observe as close to that time as possible.  Record the actual time along with other conditions in an observing log (below).   Write down notes describing the twinkling of about three stars at different altitudes and of any bright planets (e.g., Jupiter).  A good mix of stars would have altitudes of 10, 20, and 30 degrees, or perhaps 15, 30 and 45 degrees.   Make notes about the frequency and amplitude of twinkling, as well as the presence of colors changes.

YOUR REPORT

Your Introduction should include a description of scintillation and its relation to seeing and the atmosphere.

Your Results should include your tables of observations and weather data.  You should plot as many of your measureables vs time as possible so that you can spot correlations between twinkling and things like wind speed and pressure change.  When you think you see a quantity that correlates with twinkling, you should plot one against  the other (e.g., twinkling on y-axis, wind speed on the x-axis).

In your Conclusions, consider these questions:
1) Was it easy to notice differences in twinkling from one night to the next, and if so, what was the best way to quantify it?
2) Both seeing and twinkling have to do with the inhomogeneities in the atmosphere.  Do they correlate?
3) Somehow, the Canadian Meteorological Center forecasts the seeing, which implies that it must correlate with approaching weather conditions.  Did you find a weather condition that was conducive to both good seeing and low twinkling?
