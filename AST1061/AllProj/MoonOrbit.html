Project #1:  The orbital periods of the Moon
DESCRIPTION:  You will observe the Moon's phase and position (relative to the stars) around the 1st quarter phase of the Moon for at least two months in a row.  You can then measure for yourself the length of the synodic and sidereal months.  The synodic month is the time between two like phases.  The sidereal month is the time between two passages of the Moon past a given position on the sky (use right ascension because the Moon will not follow the exact same path on consecutive months).  Therefore, you must note the precise phase (% illumination) of the Moon and its position in the sky (plot it on a star map) on the 1st quarter and the 4 days before and after 1st quarter.

INSTRUCTIONS:

You will consult a planetarium program (e.g., Stellarium) to plan your observations.   You want to observe the Moon within 5 days of 1st quarter and during the hours between 7 pm and midnight (unless morning dark hours are better for you).   You will then bring a star map outside to mark the position of the Moon on the map as precisely as you can.  At some point, try to estimate the uncertainties in your Moon positions (in degrees) and your % illumination (as a %).  Also, mark on a log (example below) the relevant information for each siting.  This includes the Date and Time of your sitings (cols. 1, 2), the place where you were standing (col 3), the percentage of the sky covered with clouds and how visible the Moon was (col. 4), what percentage of the moon appeared illuminated (col. 5), the percent illumination as predicted on some web site or almanac (col. 6), whether you made a mark on your map or not (col. 7), and the equatorial coordinates of the Moon (cols. 8 and 9) -- this can be determined after the fact while comparing your map to a planetarium program.

Your goal is to measure the amount of time between two consecutive like-phases, and two consecutive passages of a given right ascension.  For example, if you saw the Moon at 45% illumination in February and then again in March, you calculate how many days (to the nearest tenth of a day) passed between them.  That gives you the synodic period.  Similarly, if you marked the time that the Moon passed the 12h 30m line of right ascension (RA) in February and then again in March, that difference in time would be (approximately) the sidereal month.  The RA and % illumination you choose don't matter, so long as they are matched.

Unless you are very lucky, at least half of your nights will be clouded out.  Thus, you may have to use interpolation or other clever approaches to figure out the length of the sidereal and synodic months.  For example, you measure the 45% illumination date in February, but only the 40% and 50% dates in March, you could take the date half-way between the 40% and 50% times.

Your introduction should include a description of the Moon's orbit, such as its eccentricity, its inclination relative to the ecliptic plane, and the synodic and sidereal periods.  You should also explain why there is a difference between the synodic and sidereal periods (paraphrase your textbook).

Your results should include a plots of Moon RA vs time, Phase vs time, and possibly Declination vs time.  Those plots can be used for interpolation.

In your conclusions, consider these questions:
1) When the Moon reached the same RA, was its Declination higher or lower than before?  How do you expect the Declination at a given RA to change throughout the year?
2) During which phases does the % illumination of the Moon change the fastest from one day to the next?  Was the 1st quarter phase a good choice for measuring the synodic period of the Moon?
3) When you "connect the dots" on your star map, is the Moon's path smooth or does it show irregularities?  Using Stellarium to animate the Moon's motion across the sky should be revealing;  can you explain the wiggling motion of the Moon with a period of 1 day?
