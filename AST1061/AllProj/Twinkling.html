Project #5:  The Weather and Twinkling

UNDER CONSTRUCTION
DESCRIPTION:   When stars twinkle, it is not something happening physically to the star, but instead it is related to the inhomogeneities of the Earth's atmosphere.  You will record the weather conditions for every night (don't use forecasts, only current conditions) and observe twinkling at least 2 times per week.   These times should not be closer together than a day unless the weather changes drastically between observations.   Then you will look for correlations between the amount of twinkling and the weather.  Is twinkling more extreme when the sky is completely clear or when it is hazy?  Does it occur more when temperatures are high, or low, or when temperatures are climbing or falling?  When is twinkling the least noticeable?  Since twinkling is hard to quantify, you should also record the seeing forecasts given on this web page:  http://cleardarksky.com/c/ONUObOHkey.html?1 for the nights that you can observe.  Can you see a correlation between seeing and twinkling?

INSTRUCTIONS:

Record the weather for every evening around the time you will observe.  This should include temperature, temperature rise or fall since previous night, wind, pressure, and change in pressure since previous night. In addition, record the seeing on a scale of 1 to 5, and the cloud cover (in % coverage) using the Clear Sky Chart for ONU (http://cleardarksky.com/c/ONUObOHkey.html?1 ). You should be able to get by without records for every night, but try to get as complete records as possible in order to notice correlations.

Go outside to observe twinkling about 2 times per week.  If you plan to check the sky 4 nights a week, on average, you should be able to see the sky about 2 times per week.  Twinkling is difficult to quantify without instruments.  With a photometer, one could quantify the magnitude of the fluctuations (scintillation) and also the time between large fluctuations.  Since you don't have such instruments, you may be able to develop a way to characterize the twinkling after watching it on a few different nights, and at different altitudes above the horizon.  You'll see that twinkling is most noticeable with bright stars at low altitudes.  Planets usually do not twinkle, but they can under extreme conditions, so you should check the planets for twinkling as they can indicate the strongest twinkling conditions.  If the twinkling seems to happen just once in awhile, try measuring the time between strong twinkles.  That average time could be a way to characterize it.
