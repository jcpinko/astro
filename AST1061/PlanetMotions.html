Project #4:  The Orbits of Planets and Comets
DESCRIPTION:  You will see for yourself why planets were called "wandering stars" - they are bright dots that move relative to the constellations.  You will record the positions of two solar system bodies and try to make sense of their motion over a 2-3 month period.  For spring 2013, Jupiter is the obvious choice, although Saturn will be visible before midnight by mid-February and Mars and Venus are early evening or morning objects.  Also, Comet PANSTARRS (C2011 L4) should reach naked-eye brightness around March/April.  You should begin by observing the two planets with the best visibility (bright and above the horizon at a convenient time).  If a comet becomes naked-eye visible, you can drop the planet which is least visible and try to focus on the comet.  Binoculars (stored at the observatory) can greatly improve the visibility of comets, allowing you to record them even as they have faded from naked-eye view.

INSTRUCTIONS:

You will be measuring the positions of planets in a few ways.  First, altitude and azimuth can be estimated by calibrating your fist held at arms length as an angle measuring tool.  For altitude, you can do a bit better if you construct an "astrolabe" out of string, tape, a nut, a protractor and a straw.  For azimuth, some tripods actually have an azimuth readout that can be used.  Second, right ascension and declination should be measured relative to the stars.  You can do this by marking the position of the planet carefully on a star map (like those provided for constellations).  Later, using either the grid on the star map, or a planetarium program, figure out the coordinates of that mark.  It is preferred that you turn the planets "off" on the program so as not to bias your judgement.  (Approach this as if you are checking the accuracy of the planetarium program.  It certainly needs its orbital elements updated monthly for comets and asteroids!)

YOUR REPORT

Your Introduction should include a description of the orbits of planets, Kepler's laws, and retrograde motion.

Your Results should include plots of your objects motion throughout the semester.  You should show a star map that includes the ecliptic and mark the points where you saw the object.  You can also plot separately the object's RA vs time and DEC vs time.   Even better, plot the objects ecliptic longitude and latitude vs time.  Make it clear which points are your observations and which came from the planetarium program.  

In your Conclusions, consider these questions:
1) Did your objects show any retrograde motion?  If not, why?  Did they show a change in speed?
2) What was the uncertainty of your positional measurements?  How do they compare to Tycho Brahe's?
3) Can you measure the inclination of your objects' orbits from your data?
4) Compare and contrast the motion of the comet to that of a planet.
