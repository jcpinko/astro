Project #3:  Tracing the Sun's Analemma
DESCRIPTION:  You will observe the Sun's position (Altitude and Azimuth) at the same time of day (sticking to standard time) for about twice per week for at least 3 months.  You will record the altitude and azimuth of the Sun at exactly the same time of day for as many days as possible.  Choose a time to record that is within 2 hours of noon. Once a week will probably be a sufficient frequency, but observations should be sustained over about 3 months.  If you have a good method for measuring the Sun's position (a makeshift gnomon, or sundial) you will easily notice the change in altitude of the Sun as Winter progresses into Spring.  But you will also see a change in azimuth.  (This is related to the "equation of time"; the way the solar day increases and decreases throughout the year.  This in turn, is related to the Earth's elliptical orbit and its axis tilt.) You will plot your positions on a graph of altitude and azimuth.  An alternative to making measurements on a piece of paper is to take wide-field photos. (You will hurt your eyes focusing on a zoomed image of the Sun, so be careful!!)  You must take your pictures with your camera in the same position - use a tripod.  The photos can then be combined into a composite photo or made into an animation. Try to explain the way the Sun's noontime position changes in terms of the Earth's orbital motion and axial tilt.

INSTRUCTIONS:

You really need a precise way to make the positional measurements of the Sun.  Measuring altitude and azimuth using one's fist at arms length will not quite be precise enough.  In fact, even an astrolabe will probably not be precise enough, and it will only provide altitude.  So here are some ways to make better measurements: 
1) A board with a stick mounted perpendicularly to it.  Such a stick is called a "gnomon".  Put a piece of paper on the board such that, when the board is laid flat on the ground, the shadow of the stick will fall on the paper and can be marked at the same time every day.  (The board + gnomon makes a sundial.) The position of the board, its levelness, and the gnomon's position have to be repeated closely from one day to the next.  A bubble-level may be useful.  Rulers can be used to measure the height, h, and the length of the shadow, l, so that the altitude will be given by a = tan-1 (h/l). 
2) Use the board and paper from example 1, but instead of making a stick, find some natural corner on campus (e.g., the edge of a brick wall, or the corner of a railing) that is permantly fixed to use as the gnomon.  Place the board in the region where the shadow will be at your measurement times.  Discretely mark (with chalk) the edges of your board so you can repeat its position.  Note: if you use something too tall, (like a flagpole) the shadow will change its position from January to April so much that it may not fit on the board.  As a rule of thumb, expect the shortest and the longest shadow to differ in length by about 2/3 of the height of the "gnomon".   A 3-foot high railing would be good.
3) Use a wide-field digital camera on a tripod.  The tripod foot positions on the ground would have to be marked for future use.   The direction that the camera is pointing (both altitude and azimuth) need to stay fixed, as does the zoom on any zoom lens.  Then the Sun will change its position relative to the fixed scenery and relative to the pixels on your images.  The change in pixel position can be converted into a change in altitude and azimuth.  Avoid looking through the camera at the Sun - you can use trial and error to find which pointing position puts the Sun near the center.  (But try to adjust for the fact that the Sun will be changing its position while the camera stays fixed.)  Later, you can use software to combine all of the images.  That is how pictures like this are made:


Of the three previous methods, I think that #2 would be the easiest to carry out.  You can come up with another method, just describe it well in your procedure.

You must also know the time precisely and measure at the same time to within about 1 minute. (The Sun moves about 1/4 degree per minute, but the Sun's diameter is only 1/2 degree.)  So make sure that your timepiece does not get too far ahead or behind.  This project has been done photographically before over an entire year by many people (try a Google image search for "analemma").  You will only be able to trace out about 1/4 of this "figure 8" in 3 months.
