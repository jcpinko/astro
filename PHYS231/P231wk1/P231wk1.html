    The above are "Base" units because all other units are derived from them.  Example: the Joule (J) is a unit of energy derived from kg, m, and s.  1 J = 1 kg m2/s2.   So the Joule is a derived unit.

  B) Standards - a practical, tangible definition of a unit.

  • Length standard (1960) - the meter was the distance between two lines on a specific platinum-iridium bar stored under controlled conditions in France.
  • Length standard (today) - the meter is the distance traveled by light in vacuum during a time of 1/299792458 second.
  • Mass standard (today) - the kilogram is the mass of a specific platinum iridium alloy cylinder kept at Sevres, France.


  C) Closely related to units are dimensions.

  • The word "Quantity" in the above tables could be replaced by  "dimension". 
  • The dimensions for the base units kg, m, and s are mass, length, and time, respectively.
  • The dimension of velocity, v, is length per time.
  • It is not possible for a formula or equation to be correct if the dimensions are different on the left and right of the equal sign. Thus, dimensional analysis is a useful tool for checking your work in science.

(Dictionary definition: dimension: (Phys.) The manifoldness with which the fundamental units of time, length, and mass are involved in determining the units of other physical quantities.)

III. Significant figures and errors in measurements

          A) Distinguish between accuracy and precision

    1. Accuracy: how close the measurement is to the "true" value

    2. Precision: how repeatable the measurement is with the given instrumental setup

B) Basic rules of significant digits.

      a) it is a mistake to write down an answer with an excessive number of digits – only one digit should be uncertain, the least significant digit (LSD)
      b) the LSD is the rightmost digit in a number like "1.234" and the rightmost non-zero digit in a number like "1234000" (it is 4 in both cases.)
      c) a decimal point on a number greater than 1 implies that the 1's place is significant, even if it is "0". (E.g., 1234000. has all digits significant.)
      d) when writing an error, there should be only 1 significant figure.
      e) in most homework and quiz problems of this course, it is ok to show 3 significant figures unless directed otherwise.

C) How to determine errors (aka uncertainties) from significant digits?

      a) There is NO universally accepted rule for deriving errors from significant digits, however, ...
      b) The least significant digit (LSD) vaguely suggests an error.
         Ex) 3.215 m implies anything from 3.215+-0.0005 m to 3.215+-0.005 m.
                I prefer +-0.001 (a one in the least significant place).
                Many fields (engineering) prefer +-0.0005.  (a 0.5 in the least significant place).
      c) It is always better to explicity show uncertainties with the “+-” notation

D) Error Propagation: when many measurements are combined mathematically, the final answer must have an error that depends on the input errors.

a) Rigorous methods exist (we won't cover them here) 

b) If no errors shown,
    i) multiplication or division: the final answer should have the same number of significant digits as the individual measurement with the smallest number of significant digits.
    ii) addition or subtraction: the final answer should have its LSD in the same decimal place as the least precise individual measurement

c) We will approximate the error by “adding the percentages”.

     Example: find volume of a box with sides a=1.000+-.002 cm, b=6.0+-0.3 cm, c=10.0+-0.4 cm.

The corresponding percentage errors are 0.2% 5% 4%
The volume is, V=1x6x10 = 60 cm3,
the percentage error is 0.2%+5%+4 % = 9.2%. 0.092 x 60 cm3 = 5.52 cm3
the final error must have 1 significant figure: V = 60 +- 6 cm3

d) How to determine the error for a measurement (e.g., one of the side lengths in the above example).

      • Estimate instrumental precision from size of smallest “tickmarks”,

      • Consider the ease of use of the instrument (e.g., a shaking hand worsens the precision of a ruler)

      • Repeat the measurement many times

      • Consider systematic errors (e.g., the instrument is mis-calibrated), add to instrumental error accordingly.  (Takes good judgement.)
