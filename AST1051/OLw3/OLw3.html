Review and Discussion of Ch. 6: The Solar System  (answers only)

  1. Types of objects in the solar system include: a star (the Sun), solar wind (particles), gas planets, Earth-like planets, life forms, moons, ring systems, asteroids, comets, and Kuiper Belt objects.
  2. Comparative planetology is comparing and contrasting the properties of the diverse worlds we encounter -to understand better the formation and evolution of planets.  It is useful in that it provides numerous testing grounds for planetary formation hypotheses, rather than having just one example to test ones hypotheses.  Its ultimate goal is to explain planetary evolution.  For many (geologists, meteorologists, ecologists) the goal is to better understand the Earth.

  3. One needs to know the distance to a planet if determining its mass from the orbits of its moons.  The orbital period and the maximum angular separation of the moon away from the planet can be measured directly, but finding the mass will require one to know the average distance between the planet and its moon.  This planet-moon distance can be found if we know the Earth-planet distance.  Dp-m/De-p = angle.  Then use Newton's form of Kepler's 3rd law to solve for the total mass.
  4. Ways the solar system is orderly: planets are revolve CCW as seen from north, planets orbits fall nearly in the same plane, dense worlds lie close to the Sun while less dense worlds lie far from the Sun. 
  5. Ways the solar system is disorderly:  the rotation axes of planets point in many different directions, Pluto does not fit neatly into the two main groups of planets.

  6. The Terrestrial planets are Earth, Mercury, Venus and Mars.  The prefix "terra" means "earth", so the name implies that they are Earth-like planets.

  7. The Jovian planets are Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune.  The word origin is "Jove" which is the same Roman diety after which Jupiter was named. Thus, these planets are Jupiter-like.
  8. Important differences between Jovians and terrestrials?
    • size/mass
    • composition
    • distance from Sun

  9. Pluto has a size, mass, and surface like the terrestrial planets, but its distance from the sun and composition are Jovian.  Thus it is difficult to put Pluto in either category unless we decide that one particular property is the determinant.

  10. Asteroids and meteoroids are important because they formed close to the time of the solar systems formation.  Meteoroids can be fragments of asteroids, but most of them are small and originate in comets.  The asteroid fragments can reach Earth's surface where we can analyze them. 
    These objects are important for another reason: the pose a potential hazard to all of life on Earth should a large one crash into the Earth.
  11. We know of the composition of comets from ground-based spectroscopy.  This is most effective for learning the constituents of the gas tails.  The dust particles are more difficult to discern with spectroscopy.  Recently, however, actual cometary material has been probed and gathered by satellites such as "Stardust" and "Deep Impact".

  13. Scientists use gravity assists in order to save on fuel weight and cost.  They plan the trajectories of the spacecraft to pass behind planets as they pass by. The planet pulls on the spacecraft, speeding it up and giving it energy.

  14. All of the planets have been visited by Earth except Pluto.  Some of the visits were just "fly-bys", while Venus, Mars, Jupiter (arguably, because it has no solid surface) and some moons (the Moon, Titan), have been landed upon.

  15. Galileo and Cassini took circuitous routes in order to take advantage of gravity assists by other planets.  This saved on the weight and cost of fuel. Voyager and Pioneer also took advantage of gravity assists, but the computation of orbits was not as fast and precise as it is today.

  18. The condensation theory explains the compositional differences of the Terrestrials and Jovians.  The terrestrials were in a hot part of the nebula where only high-melting point materials (iron-based, silicates) could condense.  At larger distances, ices of water, carbon dioxide, ammonia could also condense.   Thus, the composition of the Jovians has much less abundent iron-based minerals, for example.  Also, since the lower melting point ices are also lower in density, the density of the Jovians should be lower.  The gaseous molecules are more abundant in the solar nebula. So this also explains why the Jovian protoplanets would be larger than the terrestrial ones.  (The greater size and mass of Jovians also came about because they were more capable of capturing gas from the surrounding nebula.)  

  19. (See above)  Factors contributing to large mass of Jovians: more materials were able to condense at the lower temperatures, more massive protoplanet led to stronger gravity and thus more material attracted, the lower temperatures and large mass enable the Jovians to hold onto their atmospheres (whereas the same gas would escape from the surface of Mercury).
